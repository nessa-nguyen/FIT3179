Current Active Volcano Locations

This map visualisation shows the locations for all of the current active volcano locations. Interestingly, most of the volcanoes are aligned with the separations between the tectonic plates. This is because the gaps between the tectonic plates allow the magma to easily rise up to the surface of earth. These locations are called the subduction zone.

Frequency of Volcanic Explosivity Index (VEI)

The visualisation on the left shows the frequency of a specific VEI. The VEI is a measure of explosiveness where 0 is non-explosive,4 is a very large explosion and 8 is almost catastrophic. The Yellowstone eruption from approximately 600,000 years ago would have had a VEI of 8, with the plume being much greater than 20 kilometres. From the data, it is clear that a VEI of two is the most common by far. This VEI is described as explosive and generally has a plume of around one to five kilometres.

Volcanic Explosivity Index vs. Elevation of Volcanoes

This visualisation shows the correlation between the elevation of volcanoes and the VEI. These correlations are more noticeable when comparing specific types of volcanoes. An example of this is the submarine volcano type. This volcano type has a low elevation and low VEI. In comparison to a pyroclastic shield volcano which has a moderate elevation of around 1km and a VEI of around 3-4.
